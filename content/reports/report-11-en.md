## Introduction

The AI landscape is witnessing a transformative shift where compute resources are becoming the new form of subsidies. As the battle for dominance intensifies, major tech companies are offering startups substantial compute resources for free, reminiscent of past strategies employed by internet firms. This development, coupled with evolving expectations in semiconductor performance and long-term data center contracts, is reshaping how we perceive and invest in artificial intelligence.

## Free Compute: The New Subsidy

In the second week of July 2026, reports highlighted a growing trend among AI giants to offer free compute resources to startups. This strategy, involving the provision of cloud credits and access to AI models, aims to lock in these startups early. In essence, by alleviating initial server costs, these tech behemoths are not only securing potential long-term clients but also creating a high barrier to switching platforms later on.

The rationale behind this approach is clear: the customer lock-in effect in AI can be stronger than in traditional software. Once a startup builds its data pipelines, security settings, and model deployment on a specific cloud platform, the cost of transitioning becomes substantial. This mirrors strategies used by telecom companies that offered phone subsidies in exchange for long-term contracts.

This shift in investment dynamics is significant for venture capitalists as well. Previously, startups would secure funding which would then trickle down to cover cloud costs. Now, cloud companies are directly subsidizing these expenses, effectively absorbing some of the venture capital roles. This means that while startups may show impressive revenue growth, the underlying unit economics could remain unproven without these subsidies.

## Samsung's Performance and AI Semiconductor Expectations

The semiconductor industry faced turbulence this week following Samsung Electronics' earnings report. Despite improved profits driven by AI demand, the report fell short of heightened market expectations, causing a ripple of uncertainty among semiconductor stocks. This indicates a shift in market sentiment where absolute earnings are overshadowed by expectations and supply chain positioning.

In the AI semiconductor cycle, the focus has moved from merely selling memory to securing key customer approvals in HBM and advanced packaging. Samsung's exploration of hybrid bonding technology, which promises improved power efficiency, is a pivotal factor that could influence its future market position.

The current market adjustment doesn't signal the end of AI investments, but rather a more discerning approach by investors. Companies like NVIDIA continue to see strong demand for accelerators, but the distribution of profits across semiconductor firms is becoming more selective, depending on where bottlenecks occur within the AI value chain.

## Data Centers and Infrastructure Investments

Another significant development this week was the announcement of a 20-year, $19 billion data center lease agreement between TeraWulf and Anthropic. This underscores the shift in AI costs from model development to infrastructure and power contracts. The ability to secure necessary computational power has become a precondition for competitive AI model deployment.

This transformation alters the accounting structure for AI companies. Unlike traditional software firms, AI businesses incur ongoing costs for running servers and consuming power whenever a user interacts with the model. Therefore, AI companies' competitiveness now hinges not only on model accuracy but also on inference costs, power rates, and long-term infrastructure agreements.

## Corporate Movements: A Closer Look

### Samsung Electronics

Despite Samsung Electronics' profit gains, the market's disappointment highlights the importance of not just meeting earnings expectations but also securing stable customer relationships in HBM supply. Future success for Samsung hinges on demonstrating customer approval, production stability, and power efficiency improvements.

### NVIDIA

NVIDIA's focus on synthetic data generation for financial AI research reflects its strategy to dominate industry-specific AI tools. By integrating software layers like NVIDIA NeMo, NVIDIA ensures prolonged demand for its GPUs, binding enterprises to its ecosystem.

### Neuromeka

Neuromeka's development of the zero-shot AI humanoid robot, EIR, targets the physical AI domain, where adaptability to unforeseen situations is crucial. The company's pursuit of KC certification indicates its commitment to ensuring safety and reliability in environments shared with humans.

### Nous Research

Nous Research's release of the open-source coding model, NousCoder-14B, poses a challenge to paid coding agents. By offering a cost-effective alternative with sufficient performance, Nous Research encourages enterprises to consider self-hosted solutions, potentially altering the demand for proprietary coding models.

### Meta

Meta's improved stock performance reflects renewed optimism in its AI strategy, driven by its vast user base and robust ad revenue. The challenge remains in proving that AI investments can enhance ad efficiency and user engagement, thereby justifying the heavy spending.

## Conclusion

The AI industry is at a crossroads where free compute resources and infrastructure investments are pivotal to future success. As companies navigate this landscape, the focus is shifting from mere model development to securing long-term computational and infrastructural capabilities. This week's developments emphasize that the AI economy is entering a phase where its financial narratives are increasingly intertwined with tangible asset management and strategic resource allocation.
